AI Literacy Capstone — Workforce Trends, APAC Focus

Adoption raced ahead. Trust didn’t keep up.

PwC’s Global Workforce Hopes & Fears Survey 2025 (~50,000 workers, 48 countries) and BCG’s AI at Work series (APAC 2025 + Global 2026, ~16,000 workers) were built independently — different teams, different questions, different years. Read side by side, they tell one story: the technology gap has closed faster than the trust gap, and that gap is now the main constraint on value.

Six tensions, read across both reports

Each card pairs a finding from PwC’s global workforce survey with a finding from BCG’s AI-at-work series. Neither report was designed to validate the other — the convergence is the signal.

T1

The silicon ceiling cracked — the support ceiling didn’t

Frontline employees caught up on adoption in a single year. Leadership guidance on what to do with the time AI frees up did not catch up with them.

PwCOnly 56% of workers feel it’s safe to try new approaches at work — and non-managers report notably lower psychological safety than managers.
BCGFrontline regular AI use jumped 51% → 74% in a year, but 66% of frontline users still get limited or no guidance on what to do with the time saved, vs 52% of leaders.
For leaders: the access gap is closed. The direction-setting gap is now the bottleneck — and it sits with managers, not IT.
T2

Trust isn’t a culture nicety — it’s a multiplier on everything else

Where trust in leadership is highest, AI adoption, optimism, and motivation all move together. Where it’s lowest, the same AI tools land very differently.

PwCWorkers with the highest trust in top management are 63% more motivated; those with the highest trust in their direct manager are 72% more motivated.
BCGFrontline employees with clear leadership support on GenAI are +31pp more likely to be regular AI users and +34pp more likely to be positive about their AI-driven career prospects — yet only 31% of APAC frontline staff report that support.
For leaders: the same AI rollout, in a low-trust team, produces anxiety; in a high-trust team, it produces a career story. The tool isn’t the variable — the manager is.
T3

Strategy beats tools — by a wide margin

Both reports independently land on the same conclusion: communicating a clear direction matters more than expanding tool access.

PwCEmployees who feel most aligned with leadership’s goals are 78% more motivated than those least aligned — yet only 64% say they understand their organisation’s goals at all.
BCGEmployees with strong strategic clarity but limited tool access report measurable AI impact at 80% — almost matching the 83% for strong clarity + strong tools, and far ahead of the 60% for limited clarity + strong tools.
For leaders: if budget is finite, fund the narrative before the next licence renewal. Clarity adds ~25pp on its own; tools alone add ~5pp.
T4

Skills anxiety has stopped being a training problem

The upskilling gap hasn’t moved in two years — because the underlying ask has changed from “learn the tool” to “redefine the role.”

PwCJust 51% of non-managers feel they have the L&D resources they need, vs 72% of senior executives. Workers who feel supported to upskill are 73% more motivated.
BCG88% believe they’ll need major upskilling within five years; only 36% feel properly trained — identical to 2025. Meanwhile 72% say AI has changed the skills their role requires, and 47% say their role has shifted toward managing/directing AI rather than doing the work.
For leaders: more training hours on the same role definition won’t close this. The role itself needs redesigning first — then the training pathway.
T5

The APAC paradox: highest adoption, highest fear

APAC workers are using AI more than almost anyone — and worrying about their jobs more than almost anyone, at every level of seniority.

PwCIn Singapore, only 35% of workers are optimistic about the future of their role, vs 53% globally — despite daily GenAI use (31%) running well above the global average.
BCG52% of APAC workers fear losing their job to AI within ten years, vs 41% globally — and this fear is consistent across frontline staff (+17pp vs global), managers (+9pp) and leaders (+7pp).
For leaders: in APAC, “they’re already using it, so they must feel fine about it” is the wrong read. Adoption and anxiety are rising together — explored further in Localization, below.
T6

AI makes work better and harder — and governance hasn’t caught up with agents

The “joy paradox”: most regular users report more satisfaction, but a large minority also report more cognitive load — and the next wave (agents) arrives before the operating model is ready.

PwCWorkers with the highest psychological safety are 72% more motivated; only 54% say their team treats failure as a learning opportunity — the foundation agentic AI will need most.
BCG67% of regular AI users report increased job satisfaction, but 41% also report increased mental strain (frontline 37%, leaders 48%). 50% say their company has no clear guidance for managing human×AI teams.
For leaders: the “honeymoon” (novelty + cognitive stretch) fades within months. What sustains it — per BCG — is strategic clarity, not the next feature release.

SG/IN

Same AI curve, opposite starting points

“Localize for APAC” usually flattens nine very different countries into one slide. Singapore and India sit at opposite ends of the same adoption curve — both near the top for AI use, but for almost opposite emotional reasons. The activation playbook has to be different.

India isn’t short on enthusiasm or adoption — it’s the highest of either. The risk BCG flags is structural: APAC firms invest less in Reshape/Invent than global leaders, so fast-moving frontline usage can outpace the operating model built to support it.
What this means for activation: in Singapore, the leadership behaviour that moves the needle is explicit, repeated reassurance — naming job security and the AI roadmap in the same breath, and closing the trust gap through visible manager follow-through (T2, T5). In a hyper-growth market like India, the more urgent move is channelling existing enthusiasm into Reshape/Invent investment and standing up human×AI governance before scale outpaces it, not after (T3, T6). Same six tensions, different entry point — the argument for a regional COE model that can flex the sequence rather than apply a single global template.

Sources & methodology

Two independently fielded surveys, read together for thematic convergence — not statistically merged.

PwC Global Workforce Hopes and Fears Survey 2025 — ~49,800 respondents, 48 countries, 28 sectors, fielded Jul–Aug 2025. Global report · Singapore edition (n=1,051).

BCG AI at Work — Is Asia Pacific Leading the Way? (APAC edition, October 2025) — n=4,559 across Australia, China, India, Indonesia, Japan, Malaysia, Singapore, South Korea, Thailand.

BCG AI at Work — Strategy Matters More Than Tools (Global, Fourth Edition, June 2026) — n=11,749 across 14 markets.

Figures are quoted as published. Where a chart value was read visually from a source PDF rather than stated in body text, it is marked with “~”. Country-level cuts for India in the BCG APAC edition include India’s contribution to the BCG Global 2025 sample (n=1,031), per BCG’s own methodology note. This dashboard was built as a hands-on AI-literacy exercise: extracting findings from four source documents, identifying cross-report themes, and translating them into a leadership-behaviour and regional-activation framework using Claude.
